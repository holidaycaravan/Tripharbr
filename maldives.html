About Maldives

A Nation of 1,200 Islands and Zero Stress

The Maldives is one of those rare destinations that actually lives up to every photograph you've ever seen. An archipelago of 1,200 coral islands in the Indian Ocean, it straddles the equator with a grace that is hard to articulate. The water is a colour that doesn't quite exist anywhere else on earth — a layered turquoise that shifts from jade to cobalt as the seabed drops away. And above it, overwater villas on stilts have made the Maldives the global symbol of luxury travel.

For Indian honeymooners and couples, the Maldives represents something very specific: complete disconnection. The resorts — spread across private islands accessible only by speedboat or seaplane — are designed around the idea of having everything you need and nothing you don't. Your villa's private deck hangs over the lagoon. Your breakfast arrives on a float. The spa is over the water. The sunset is uninterrupted. There are no crowds, no city noise, no traffic. Just light, water, and each other.

But the Maldives isn't only for honeymooners. The underwater world is one of the planet's most extraordinary — manta rays, whale sharks, sea turtles, and reef sharks are regular sightings for snorkellers and divers. Local island tours show you a traditional Maldivian life that is entirely different from the resort bubble. Glass-bottom boat trips, dolphin cruises, sunset fishing, and night snorkelling add layers to what can otherwise seem like a purely passive holiday.

When to Go

Best Time to Visit the Maldives — Month by Month

The Maldives has two seasons: the dry northeast monsoon (Nov–Apr, ideal) and the southwest monsoon (May–Oct, rainier but still enjoyable with lower prices).

January
Perfect
Peak dry season. Crystal clear water. No rain. Exceptional visibility for snorkelling. Book early — resorts fill up fast.
February
Ideal
Best month for whale shark sightings. Clear skies and calm seas. Manta ray season in full swing.
March
Ideal
Warm, calm, clear. Excellent for honeymooners. Bioluminescent plankton glows blue at night on many beaches.
April
Excellent
End of dry season. Beautiful weather. Manta rays and whale sharks still common. Slightly lower prices start.
May
Good Value
Wet season begins. Some afternoon showers. Prices drop significantly. Still very enjoyable — rain is usually brief.
June
Budget Pick
Lower prices, fewer tourists. Some rainy days but also long sunny spells. Good for budget-conscious couples.
July
Wet Season
Regular heavy rain and strong winds. Water visibility reduced. Some water sports cancelled. Not ideal.
August
Wet Season
Wettest period. Very rough seas possible. Some resorts reduce staff. Only for the budget-focused traveller.
September
Improving
Rains beginning to ease. Prices still low. Excellent budget window if weather is acceptable to you.
October
Season Starts
Dry season returns. Seas calm down. Water clarity improving rapidly. Great honeymoon month at lower prices.
November
Excellent
Dry season firmly established. Beautiful conditions. Manta ray season begins. Our favourite month for couples.
December
Peak Season
Christmas and New Year — magical in the Maldives. Perfect weather. Book 4–6 months ahead for December travel.
Travel Tips

What You Need to Know Before You Go

Visa & Arrival
The Maldives gives a free 30-day tourist visa on arrival to Indian nationals — no application needed, just your passport and return ticket.
No visa application required — simply arrive and get stamped
Arrive at Malé's Velana International Airport, then speedboat/seaplane to resort
We coordinate your resort transfer timing with your flight arrival
Seaplane transfers (for some resorts) only operate in daylight hours — plan accordingly
Choosing Your Resort
This is the most important decision in your Maldives trip. We help you choose the right resort for your budget and expectations.
Overwater villas cost more but the experience is unparalleled for honeymooners
Beach villas offer private beachfront access at lower prices — still beautiful
All-inclusive is often best value — food and drinks are very expensive à la carte
We've visited the resorts we recommend — genuine recommendations, not commissions
Cultural Notes
The Maldives is an Islamic country. Resorts on private islands operate differently from inhabited islands.
Bikinis and swimwear are fine at your private resort island — not on local islands
Alcohol is only available at resort islands (not on local islands)
Dress modestly when visiting Malé or any inhabited island
Pork products are not available anywhere in the Maldives
Packing & Connectivity
The Maldives is warm and humid year-round. Pack light and bring good sunscreen — the equatorial sun is intense.
SPF 50+ sunscreen is essential — reef-safe sunscreen preferred
Underwater camera or GoPro — you will regret not having one
Resort Wi-Fi is generally good — data is usually expensive on resort SIMs
US Dollar is widely accepted at all resorts alongside Maldivian Rufiyaa
